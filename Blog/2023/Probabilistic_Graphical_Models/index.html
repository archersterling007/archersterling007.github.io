Variational Inference in Depth

Inference in probabilistic models is often intractable. There are algorithms that provide approximate solutions to the inference problem (e.g., marginal inference) by using subroutines that involve sampling random variables. Most sampling-based inference algorithms are instances of Markov Chain Monte-Carlo (MCMC); two popular MCMC methods are Gibbs sampling and Metropolis-Hastings. Unfortunately, these sampling-based methods have several important shortcomings. Although they are guaranteed to find a globally optimal solution given enough time, it is difficult to tell how close they are to a good solution given the finite amount of time that they have in practice. In order to quickly reach a good solution, MCMC methods require choosing an appropriate sampling technique (e.g., a good proposal in Metropolis-Hastings). Choosing this technique can be an art in itself. In this post, we are going to look at an alternative approach to approximate inference called the variational family of algorithms. The main idea of variational methods is to cast inference as an optimization problem.
